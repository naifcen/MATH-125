Chapter 1: Picturing Distributions with Graphs

Foundational Terminology

  • Individuals: The objects described by a set of data.
  • Variable: A characteristic of an individual.
  • Population: The complete set of individuals of interest.
  • Sample: A subset of the population from which we actually collect data.

Parameters vs. Statistics

Population Parameter

A measurable characteristic of a population, denoted by Greek letters (e.g., $\mu$, $\sigma$, $\rho$).

Sample Statistic

A measurable characteristic of a sample, denoted by Latin letters (e.g., $\bar{x}$, $s$, $r$).

Types of Variables

  • Categorical Variable: Assigns individuals to distinct groups. (Graphs: Pie Charts, Bar Graphs)
  • Quantitative Variable: Takes on numerical values where arithmetic operations are meaningful. (Graphs: Histograms, Stemplots)

Exploratory Data Analysis (SOCS)

When examining a distribution, always describe the following:

  • Shape: Symmetric, skewed-right, or skewed-left?
  • Outlier: Are there any values that fall far from the overall pattern?
  • Center: Where is the midpoint of the data?
  • Spread: How spread out is the data?

Chapter 2: Describing Distributions with Numbers

Measures of Center

Mean ($\bar{x}$)

The arithmetic average. Formula: $\bar{x} = \frac{\Sigma x_i}{n}$

The mean is NOT a resistant measure; it's sensitive to outliers.

Median (M)

The midpoint of a distribution. Found at position $(n+1)/2$ in an ordered list.

The median IS a resistant measure; it's not affected by outliers.

Finding the Median:

  • If 'n' is ODD, the Median is the single middle value.
  • If 'n' is EVEN, the Median is the average of the two middle values.

Measures of Variability (Spread)

Quartiles ($Q_1, Q_3$) and IQR

Quartiles divide the data into four equal parts. The Interquartile Range (IQR) measures the range of the middle 50% of the data.

Formula: IQR = $Q_3 - Q_1$

Quartiles and IQR ARE resistant measures.

Finding the Quartiles:

  • $Q_1$ (First Quartile): The median of the lower half of the data.
  • $Q_3$ (Third Quartile): The median of the upper half of the data.
  • When 'n' is odd, do not include the overall Median in the lower or upper halves when finding quartiles.

Standard Deviation (s)

The most common measure of spread, it measures the typical distance of observations from the mean. It is the square root of the variance ($s^2$).

Standard Deviation is NOT a resistant measure.

The Five-Number Summary & Boxplots

A summary of a distribution consisting of: Minimum, $Q_1$, Median, $Q_3$, Maximum.

A Boxplot is a visual representation of the five-number summary.

Identifying Outliers: The 1.5 x IQR Rule

An observation is a suspected outlier if it falls outside these boundaries:

  • Lower Bound: $Q_1 - (1.5 \times IQR)$
  • Upper Bound: $Q_3 + (1.5 \times IQR)$

Chapter 4: Scatterplots & Correlation

Explanatory & Response Variables

  • Explanatory Variable (x-axis): A variable that may explain or influence changes in a response variable.
  • Response Variable (y-axis): A variable that measures an outcome of a study.

Scatterplots

A scatterplot displays the relationship between two quantitative variables measured on the same individuals. When interpreting a scatterplot, always use the **DOFS** framework:

  • Direction: Is the association positive (lower-left to upper-right) or negative (upper-left to lower-right)?
  • Outliers: Are there any striking deviations from the overall pattern?
  • Form: Is the relationship linear, curvilinear, or something else?
  • Strength: How closely do the points follow the form? (e.g., strong, weak)

Correlation ($r$)

The correlation coefficient, $r$, is a numerical measure of the direction and strength of the **linear** relationship between two quantitative variables.

  • It is always a number between -1 and +1.
  • An $r$ value close to +1 indicates a strong positive association.
  • An $r$ value close to -1 indicates a strong negative association.
  • An $r$ value close to 0 indicates a very weak linear relationship.
  • Correlation is unitless and is not affected by changes in units of measurement.

Correlation is NOT a resistant measure; it is strongly affected by outliers. Also, **correlation does not imply causation**!

Chapter 5: Regression

The Regression Line

A regression line is a straight line that describes how a response variable ($y$) changes as an explanatory variable ($x$) changes. It is used to **predict** the value of $y$ for a given value of $x$.

$\hat{y} = a + bx$

  • $\hat{y}$ (y-hat) is the **predicted value** of the response variable.
  • **Slope ($b$):** The predicted change in $y$ for each 1-unit increase in $x$.
  • **Intercept ($a$):** The predicted value of $y$ when $x=0$.

Least-Squares Regression Line (LSRL)

This is the line that makes the sum of the squared vertical distances (residuals) of the data points from the line as small as possible. It's the most common method for fitting a line to data.

The LSRL always passes through the point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

Residuals & $r^2$

  • Residual: The difference between an observed value of $y$ and the value predicted by the regression line ($\hat{y}$).

    Residual = observed $y$ - predicted $\hat{y}$

  • Coefficient of Determination ($r^2$): The proportion (or percentage) of the variation in the values of $y$ that can be explained by the least-squares regression of $y$ on $x$. For example, an $r^2$ of 0.75 means that 75% of the variation in the response variable is accounted for by the linear model.

Cautions in Regression

  • Outliers: Some outliers, known as **influential points**, can significantly change the slope, intercept, and correlation of the regression line.
  • Extrapolation: This is the use of a regression line for prediction far outside the range of values of the explanatory variable $x$ that you used to obtain the line. Such predictions are often not accurate and should be avoided.
