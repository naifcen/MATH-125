Chapter 1: Picturing Distributions with Graphs

Foundational Terminology

  • Individuals: The objects described by a set of data.
  • Variable: A characteristic of an individual.
  • Population: The complete set of individuals of interest.
  • Sample: A subset of the population from which we actually collect data.

Parameters vs. Statistics

Population Parameter

A measurable characteristic of a population, denoted by Greek letters (e.g., μ, σ, ρ).

Sample Statistic

A measurable characteristic of a sample, denoted by Latin letters (e.g., x̄, s, r).

Types of Variables

  • Categorical Variable: Assigns individuals to distinct groups. (Graphs: Pie Charts, Bar Graphs)
  • Quantitative Variable: Takes on numerical values where arithmetic operations are meaningful. (Graphs: Histograms, Stemplots)

Exploratory Data Analysis (SOCS)

When examining a distribution, always describe the following:

  • Shape: Symmetric, skewed-right, or skewed-left?
  • Outlier: Are there any values that fall far from the overall pattern?
  • Center: Where is the midpoint of the data?
  • Spread: How spread out is the data?

Chapter 2: Describing Distributions with Numbers

Measures of Center

Mean (x̄)

The arithmetic average. Formula: x̄ = (Σxᵢ) / n

The mean is NOT a resistant measure; it's sensitive to outliers.

Median (M)

The midpoint of a distribution. Found at position (n+1)/2 in an ordered list.

The median IS a resistant measure; it's not affected by outliers.

Finding the Median:

  • If 'n' is ODD, the Median is the single middle value.
  • If 'n' is EVEN, the Median is the average of the two middle values.

Measures of Variability (Spread)

Quartiles (Q₁, Q₃) and IQR

Quartiles divide the data into four equal parts. The Interquartile Range (IQR) measures the range of the middle 50% of the data.

Formula: IQR = Q₃ - Q₁

Quartiles and IQR ARE resistant measures.

Finding the Quartiles:

  • Q₁ (First Quartile): The median of the lower half of the data.
  • Q₃ (Third Quartile): The median of the upper half of the data.
  • When 'n' is odd, do not include the overall Median in the lower or upper halves when finding quartiles.

Standard Deviation (s)

The most common measure of spread, it measures the typical distance of observations from the mean. It is the square root of the variance (s²).

Standard Deviation is NOT a resistant measure.

The Five-Number Summary & Boxplots

A summary of a distribution consisting of: Minimum, Q₁, Median, Q₃, Maximum.

A Boxplot is a visual representation of the five-number summary.

Identifying Outliers: The 1.5 x IQR Rule

An observation is a suspected outlier if it falls outside these boundaries:

  • Lower Bound: Q₁ - (1.5 × IQR)
  • Upper Bound: Q₃ + (1.5 × IQR)

Exercise 2: Numerical Summaries

Using the same data from Exercise 1.

Mean (x̄): (1231 / 15) ≈ 82.07

Median (M): n=15 (odd). Location is (15+1)/2 = 8th value. Median = 84.

Conclusion: Since the mean (82.07) is less than the median (84), the distribution is slightly skewed to the left.

Exercise 3: Five-Number Summary & Outliers

Data: `15, 41, 12, 18, 22, 25, 17, 33, 21, 28, 9, 39`

Ordered Data: `9, 12, 15, 17, 18, 21, 22, 25, 28, 33, 39, 41`

Five-Number Summary:

  • Minimum = 9
  • Q₁ = (15+17)/2 = 16
  • Median = (21+22)/2 = 21.5
  • Q₃ = (28+33)/2 = 30.5
  • Maximum = 41

IQR: 30.5 - 16 = 14.5

Outlier Check: Lower Bound = 16 - (1.5 * 14.5) = -5.75. Upper Bound = 30.5 + (1.5 * 14.5) = 52.25. There are no outliers.
